Housing and the neighbourhood

Housing and people’s surrounding neighbourhood can either mitigate or exacerbate the impact of poverty on people’s lives.1 It is well known that the UK has a shortage of suitable housing, and the housing it does have is unevenly distributed.2 Such inequalities have a direct impact on how easy certain amenities are to access for those from lower socio-economic backgrounds: transport, affordable food, healthcare and schools. Moreover there is a body of evidence that suggests the built environment also has an impact on children’s early development.3

Important dimensions of housing include chronic noise exposure, which can affect reading acquisition,4 and crowding. The latter is associated with negative school attainment, reading comprehension and spatial reasoning.5 Longitudinal studies suggest that the harms of growing up in a crowded environment can persist into later life and act as an intergenerational transmission of social inequality.6 It’s important to note that poor quality housing can also lead to increased exposure to environmental toxins and hazards which can affect children’s academic, social and emotional development.7

Beyond the immediate family home, the quality of the surrounding neighbourhood (air quality, water quality, traffic density, access to green space and play space) and the availability of essential services like schools and hospitals also have an effect on children’s early development. This operates through direct exposure, but it is also about the availability of key resources, and this can help to reduce amongst parents.8

Research has made direct links between issues of poverty, housing, and children’s health, well-being and development. Research by the Joseph Rowntree Foundation has highlighted the mutually constitutive effect that poverty has on housing circumstances and, in turn, the effect of housing upon poverty. Other research has argued for the need to give greater priority to housing in the context of understanding child poverty. Housing instability has a particularly strong effect on children’s development. Frequent moves, household disorganisation, and income volatility have all been linked to lower physical, emotional and academic outcomes.
